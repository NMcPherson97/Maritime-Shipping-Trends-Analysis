# Maritime Trade Cost Analysis Dashboard

## 1. Background and Overview

Maritime shipping is a critical component of international trade, but transportation costs can vary significantly depending on the type of cargo being moved, the operational requirements involved, and the resources required to complete each shipment. Understanding the primary drivers of shipping expenses is essential for improving profitability, allocating resources efficiently, and identifying opportunities for cost reduction.

This analysis examines maritime trade activity between 2020 and 2026 to identify the cargo segments generating the highest transportation expenses and evaluate the factors contributing to those costs. The goal was to move beyond simply reporting spending levels and instead provide actionable insights that support operational decision-making and cost optimization efforts.

By analyzing voyage costs, cargo values, and major cost components, this project provides a high-level view of where transportation resources are being concentrated and highlights areas where management should focus future efficiency initiatives.

## 2. Data Structure Overview

The dataset contains 5,000 maritime shipment records representing trade activity across multiple cargo categories and shipping routes. Each record includes shipment-level information such as cargo type, trade category, origin and destination ports, cargo value, voyage costs, and individual cost components including bunker fuel, port charges, insurance, and demurrage fees.

For reporting purposes, the data was organized around three primary business dimensions: cargo type, shipment value, and transportation cost. These dimensions allowed for comparisons between operational spending and the economic value generated by each shipment category.

The final reporting structure focused on three key questions: which cargoes generate the highest transportation costs, which cost components contribute most to overall expenses, and whether shipping expenditures are aligned with the value of the cargo being transported.

## 3. Executive Summary

The analysis found that shipping costs are highly concentrated among a relatively small number of cargo types. Crude Oil represented the single largest contributor to transportation spending, accounting for a significantly larger share of voyage costs than any other cargo category. Clean Petroleum Products and Naphtha were also among the highest-cost cargoes, indicating that energy-related shipments dominate overall transportation expenditures.

Cost component analysis revealed that bunker fuel costs are the largest driver of shipping expenses, accounting for approximately 61% of total voyage costs. Port charges represented the second-largest expense category, while insurance and demurrage costs contributed a much smaller portion of overall spending. These findings suggest that fuel-related expenses represent the greatest opportunity for cost optimization efforts.

While total spending varied considerably across cargo categories, cost-to-value analysis indicated that transportation costs remain relatively consistent as a percentage of cargo value. This suggests that the primary challenge is not inefficiency within a specific category, but rather the concentration of spending among a limited number of high-volume, high-cost cargo segments.

## 4. KPI Summary

During the analysis period, total voyage costs exceeded **$11.19 billion** across **5,000 shipments**, resulting in an average transportation cost of approximately **$2.24 million per shipment**. The average cargo value was approximately **$27.67 million**, providing context for evaluating transportation expenditures relative to the value being moved through the supply chain.

Energy-related cargoes consistently represented the largest share of transportation spending, with Crude Oil alone generating more than **$3.2 billion** in voyage costs. This concentration of spending indicates that a relatively small number of cargo categories account for a substantial portion of total transportation expenditures.

Cost component analysis showed that bunker fuel expenses represented approximately **61%** of total shipping costs, followed by port charges at roughly **25%**. Together, these two categories account for the overwhelming majority of transportation spending and therefore present the greatest opportunity for operational improvements and cost management initiatives.

## 5. Recommendations

Management should prioritize cost-reduction efforts within energy-related shipping operations, particularly those involving Crude Oil and other petroleum products. Because these cargoes account for the majority of transportation spending, even modest efficiency improvements could generate meaningful cost savings across the broader shipping portfolio.

Given that bunker fuel expenses represent the largest cost component, future analysis should focus on identifying factors that influence fuel consumption and fuel-related costs. Potential areas of investigation include route optimization, vessel utilization, shipment scheduling, and operational practices that may impact fuel efficiency.

Finally, management should continue monitoring transportation costs relative to cargo value to ensure that shipping expenditures remain aligned with business objectives. While the current analysis suggests relatively consistent cost-to-value relationships across categories, ongoing monitoring can help identify emerging inefficiencies and support more informed resource allocation decisions as trade volumes and market conditions evolve.
